One of my hypotheses for increasing the probability of innovation is to go up and down the levels of super/sub-functions. 

In other words, instead of starting with saying “Let’s make transistors for the 0s and 1s” 

You go up a level and ask “What’s right above the 0s and 1s? What buttons (super/sub-functions) are we forming from those lower buttons (sub-functions)? logic gates (XOR, OR, AND, etc.)?” 

Then “Is there space for innovation there? Can we get more for less there? Is there an “any means whatsoever” for arriving at XOR, OR, AND gates?” “Are we really restricted to just 0s and 1s or transistors or electricity or silicon?” etc. 

As you traverse these levels, going up and up, you may (or may not) be widening your search space. 

It is clear though that when you start with the end function, the entire tree of sub-functions can be called into question. 

From the higher-level programs down to the chips to the transistors to the mathematical foundations. 

You can start removing entire levels, or adding new levels. 

Even the whole assumption of discreteness in hierarchy, or of hierarchy of levels can be called into question by asking “In what way could a hierarchy limit innovation?” 

You can throw out the whole recipe and start somewhere entirely different. 

Other people made many design choices for us in the construction of this tree of functions, and were set in stone. Now we’re both grateful and stuck.

Acceleration Heuristics

A powerful idea for increasing the probability of innovating is to start from what Jacque Fresco called the ‘end function’ (end goal) 

Using the terminology I’ve been using so far, this would be what the super function gets you: what you really want to accomplish, in contrast to the sub-functions and network or arrangement of those sub-functions: how you go about accomplishing it. 

Invention is much deeper than simply building a chair. This is connected to something Fresco also discussed. He talked of how definitions can be limiting factors to innovation. When we think of a ‘chair’ we usually think of something physical with four legs, it may be wood with a cushion or plastic or any other material but there’s a general shape that springs to mind. This fixed definition can limit our exploration of all the ways in which a chair could be manifest. 

The question of pinning down what you really want to accomplish is a whole world in and of itself because you could keep tracing back the underlying reasons until you hit very fundamental problems, in which case you should probably pursue those. 

But to limit the reverse tree search, let’s say that the end function of a chair is to support a person comfortably. This definition will have to get more precise. What does ‘comfortably’ mean? An even pressure distribution? 

In the end this might lead you to conclude that you’d like to make some sort of chair that allows you to float in the air, and in order to do that your experimental explorations might venture into new dimensions that a chair designer has never even entertained. 

Fresco had a technique for broadening definitions. He said it’s not perfect and that the future will arrive at much more precise definitions, but it’s a start:

“Any means whatsoever…” (for supporting a human being, or doing anything else, etc.) 

This immediately expands our search space. We’re no longer restricted by past approaches and solutions to this specific problem. 

These kinds of heuristics may either be specified or emerge from the way such a machine is structured. It would have to be able to survey very wide search spaces.

Generativity theory in psychology is a way of predicting creative behavior.

This broad overview of science, paired with a real time feedback or update of all available information would be powerful. 

Two potential problems are: 

1. The information is being generated faster than you can consume it (Surveying the people who conduct surveys is one possible solution to this) 

2. The information you survey may not be relevant to your current problems, causing the learning not to stick (Perhaps some kind of AI that can pick up on relevance to your specific problem may be useful to implement here) 

Therein lies the power of a machine without those constraints. It can handle the information being generated at such a rapid rate, and it has no need for relevancy for the storage of the information. When the machine that automates automation is built, what could it do for our tree of knowledge? 

Abstraction is a kind of automation in that when I use the word ‘thing’, I don’t have to reference every single thing that ever existed to get across the concept of a ‘thing’ so I’ve minimized the energy it takes for me to transfer an idea to you. 

Abstraction is sort of a lever in language in that both are systems for compressing large amounts of information into small amounts of information, or emitting a small input to obtain a large output. 

Abstraction is therefore, a kind of super function or master button in communication. 

In mathematics, when I say “1+2=3” I can only talk about that instance, whereas if I replace those with variables like “a+b=c” a, b, and c could refer to all numbers, and therefore we can study that without having to check every single combination of numbers. 

We can keep going and replace the “+” with a binary operator that means doing something to 2 elements (a and b) whether that be “+, -, *, /” etc. 

In this case, the binary operator is a kind of super-function.

This machine might generate super-functions in language, compressing decades of ideas into minutes. 

What kinds of patterns might a machine like this spot? 

It could start developing its own concepts based on patterns that cross all disciplines and yet are invisible to any one or visible to only a few. 

 Then, in the same way a language learner might prioritize learning that language by looking at the most frequently used words, it may even be able to construct a list of the most frequently occurring patterns in nature and life, even coining its own terms, and sort the list by frequency. 

“What are the most frequent words by the universe?” is a question it might be able to answer. 

This might allow people to learn a lot in a short amount of time. 

Abstraction as Automation

This process of automating menial work, and then automating the upper level further, and so on, is a cycle in itself. It is a meta-cycle of progress. 

As much as programmers like to think they’re more efficient than the average human who does a repetitive task (and they are), programmers are also going through the cycle of finding things to automate, and then automating them. 

What is needed is not the programmer, but the automation. If one could create an AI that can do the job of the programmer or AI researcher, one automates the process of automation. 

We will reach a point where humans no longer partake in the 'zooming out' or time shrinking. Even that cycle (of the evolution of all fields) will be automated. 

Instead of just self-correcting machines, or self-replicating machines, we will have self-evolving and progress-accelerating machines. 

Numerous times when humans have automated cycles, there has been a co-occurrence of tremendous gains in the frequency of those cycles (the speed with which a task is completed) such as a drop in time complexity. Machines can oftentimes do it better, with fewer errors, and faster. 

When we give machines the task of automating the evolution of progress, themselves, of science, technology, and every field under the sun, imagine just how fast this process of time shrinking will accelerate. 

We go from progress spanning millennia to progress spanning moments. 

Inconceivable super-functions would start to emerge (the equivalent of flight) since this machine would plunge into every single discipline that exists, survey all available information, seeing all current and available sub-functions it could combine or merge and then assemble a tree for new super-functions. From the extremities of a deep sea anemone's neuronal cell processes to the dynamics of pulsing cepheid variable stars. 

Our learning will become enhanced because these machines will be able to reference relevant information, and translate it based on our background, developing goal-driven gradients-of-change for us on-the-fly (see: differential reinforcement and successive approximations, reusable learning objects, just-in-time teaching/learning)

Automating Automation

With the release of GPT-3, or any other advanced technology, a phenomenon occurs where people start thinking more creatively about possible pathways/options they could take. 

One reason for this might be that the feedback loop gets tighter. 

Instead of spending months building an AI system, thinking about different architectures to use, different training processes, different ways of framing the problem, finding the resources to train your AI, programming and debugging, and dealing with a plethora of unforeseen variables, finally to arrive at your end goal of finding out whether your idea works or not, you can simply think of a mapping between 2 text patterns and try it out on GPT-3 to see if ‘it gets the message’. 

Shrinking the overarching feedback loop from months to minutes. 

With a tighter feedback loop, behavior is more likely to ‘catch on’ through success. In other words the probability of a particular behavior occurring again increases. 

In the case of GPT-3, this increases the probability of different kinds of trials and experimentations at a rapid pace. At every trial, one does in minutes what would’ve taken months years ago, years decades ago, decades centuries ago. 

At every iteration of cycle automation and cycle frequency increase, feedback loops get tighter and tighter, creativity becomes abundant by the mere fact that energy expenditure has shrunk. 

Creativity becomes cheap. 

Ideas that you would’ve otherwise dismissed or thrown away due to the tremendous number of steps (including unknown steps) you had to take, now get a chance to shine due to the mere fact that it almost costs nothing to try them. 

More of your tries work than not, so the behavior doesn’t die off quite as fast as it would’ve had it cost so much energy for every trial. 

The tighter the feedback loops gets, the faster you can error correct. The faster you can error correct, the faster you can learn (get to your goal by successive approximation). 

The cheaper creativity gets, both financially and energetically, the wider the search space of super functions or solutions we can collectively traverse as a civilization. 

When creativity becomes free (meaning access to all resources), innovation will skyrocket since all old, current, and subsequent or new super functions will be available as sub-functions for everyone to use and build upon. 

Lack of collective access hurts our individual homeostasis because individual homeostasis is throttled by collective homeostasis. 

Where would we be without all the people who have contributed so much to all our lives (Pasteur, Maxwell, Einstein, Newton, Euler, and the list goes on and on…)? 

Imagine a world where none of these people had the means to experiment, learn, let alone make a living. 

As Jacque Fresco says “We should’ve had thousands of them” and this is why sharing resources is essential.

The more of these supercomputers called brains being tapped, the better.

Consequences of a shorter feedback loop

As you build super-functions on top of sub-functions the cycles done per unit of time increases. For the same master button press you can get an order of magnitude greater in cycles done per unit of time, than if you had gone down to all the sub-functions and pressed them sequentially since the master button presses all the sub-buttons simultaneously. 

Human civilization seems to be performing what AI people might call a collective ‘gradient descent’ towards energy minimization (on their part) or cycle compression in all areas of life. The history of civilization sounds like a bouncing ball that never stopped bouncing. 

If I asked you to choose between a master button that you click only once to arrive at your goal (whatever your goal may be) and 10 years of hard work at a job you dislike to arrive at your goal, which would you choose?

Most would probably choose the button. 

It may be that the majority of organisms, not just humans, are ‘searching’ for the nearest thing in real life to a master button, or super-function that you press once and reach your end goal/function. 

In programming, we write little code snippets called ‘functions’, you can think of it like a shortcut name for a list of instructions to a computer. If we had a robot say, and we wanted to tell it to enter the room, we have to first give it the instructions of moving towards the door (sub-function), raising its arm (sub-function), moving it towards the door knob (sub-function), etc. 

Finally we package all those little instructions into a master button, or super-function, called ‘Enter the room’ that way all we have to say is “enter the room” instead of all the little instructions. 

These instructions do keep going down in level. For instance moving towards the door is made up of lower-level sub-functions, you convert from one programming language to another and finally down to 0s and 1s. 

Now of course, we can trace or build the tree up or down. 

‘Enter the room’ could be a sub-function of a larger super-function that means ‘deliver food at specified times everyday’ which could be a sub-function of a larger super-function ‘Create a service schedule’ and so on. 

Imagine the history of invention, ideas, and technological progress as a tree of buttons with the sub-functions (or sub-buttons) being past ideas, and super-functions (or master buttons) are functions built from those past ideas.

Computers we build are added to our toolkit which can then be used to build better kinds of computers and technology, to make more discoveries. In futures studies, this is the concept of Accelerating change. Positive feedback seems to occur which is discussed by Kurzweil in his “Law of Accelerating Returns”
